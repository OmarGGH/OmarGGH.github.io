San Francisco Crime
What Data Tells Us

San Francisco is one of the most famous cities in the United States. Although its population does not even reach a million residents, its dynamics, diversity, tourism, and culture make it a unique urban place to live in. As many cities around the world, the human complexity translates to crime occurrences. Those occurrences has shifted dramatically over the past two decades, passing through urban change, policy shifts, financial crises, and a global pandemic.

This portfolio explores those patterns through data. It uncovers when, where, and what type of crime defines the city.

01

About the Data

In order to explore crime in a place, we first need to understand the data registered. In the case of this project, the data was sourced from the San Francisco Police Department's historical incident reports [1], covering the period between 2003 and 2025.

i

The Dataset

Two datasets were used for this project, one that covers the 2003-2018 period, and another one that covers the 2018-2025 period.

02

Trends Over Time

Crime in SF has been falling, but the story is more complex than it seems.

San Francisco's reported crime figures have a particularly change in recent years. Many crimes had very high numbers in the last years of 2010s, but then, after COVID-19 pandemic, those numbers decreased greatly. This is a pattern which was not only observed in San Francisco, but in many cities around the world [4].

Back in 2017, the San Francisco government implemented the Law Enforcement Assisted Division (LEAD) program, which focuses on a "harm reduction policing, on those who use drugs and/or engage in sex work" [5]. The goal of that policy was to reduce the arrest of individuals and instead guide them to reduce the offenses. As can be seen in the Figure 1, although a noble idea, it does not always work. Prostitution levels decreased, but the drug offense crimes increased by a great amount in recent years.

03

Where It Happens

Prostitution clusters can tell the spatial distribution of crime.

Crime does not distribute uniformly across a city. There are zones which are more prone to crime than others due to their land use, socioeconomic status, accesability to public facilities, etc. When talking about it, it is easy to fall in biases and stereotypes, but data can help to understand how crime is distributed in space.

In this particular case, prostitution was selected in order to show how crime can change through time in a geographical setting. In the case of this type of crime, a generalized reduction can be seen. The heatmap on Figure 2 shows how in the earlier years of the 2010s, this crime was very present in the eastern part of the city. In recent years, that tendency has decreased, being explained by the social policies implemented by the city government [5] [6], as well as the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

04

Crime by the Clock

Crimes also have their own schedules.

The distribution of criminal activity across the hours of the day varies a lot. As well as other human activities, crimes are shaped by human routines and it is not always true the general idea of having more crimes at night.

The treemap and sunburst visualization of Figure 3 allows exploration of both category proportions and time-of-day events. Most of the crimes have their peak occurences at afternoon and evening hours, while at the same time, the earlier hours of the day (0:00 - 6:00) are the ones with less crime (criminals also sleep, or is it because of the police?). One interesting insight is the Drug Offense category, which can be seen that it is primarily done in the afternoon hours, meaning that the crime could be seen as a recreational activity, and it could be that it is not so related to being discrete as other crimes that occur at night.
